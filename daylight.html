Context
A homeowner going solar signs one agreement and expects one clear bill. Behind that simplicity sits a regulated, multi-year financing arrangement, a forecasted billing engine, money moving between financiers, and a physical install that has to pass inspection before a single dollar is charged.

Daylight is the consumer energy platform that makes all of that feel like nothing at all to the person on the couch. I lead product across it — the homeowner app, the operator workflows, and the billing experience that ties them together.
Problem
Solar is one of the most opaque, high-trust purchases a person makes — and it runs on machinery most homeowners never see: five parties, a decade-plus billing relationship, and dozens of states where a deal can stall or a payment can fail silently.

Get it wrong and trust evaporates in a category already seen as predatory. The product challenge is to orchestrate the whole machine while showing the homeowner a single, calm, trustworthy experience.

The work

Modeling workflow states, permissions, document collection, review loops, and the operational handoffs between sales, installer, and back office — so a deal keeps moving instead of stalling.

Why it's hard

Every step has its own failure modes and its own owner. The product has to make the current state, the blocker, and the next action obvious to whoever is holding the deal right now.

Billing that just works
Billing can't start until the system is switched on and approved to operate — then it has to settle into a rhythm the homeowner can count on. The tricky part is timing: the start date and the annual cycles anchor to when the system goes live, while the monthly cadence stays fixed. I designed the experience so none of that complexity lands on the homeowner — just a predictable bill, on the same day, every month.

Designing the unhappy path
The real product work lives in the edge cases. A bank account that won't verify. A returned payment. A homeowner drifting toward delinquency. I designed the flows that catch these early, explain them in plain language, and give people a clear way back — because in a trust-heavy category, how you handle the bad day matters more than the good one.
Resilience · happy path vs. edge cases
The system has to be honest on the worst day, not just the best one.
Happy path
  • Bank verified
  • Autopay active
  • Charged on the due date
  • Payment posts, homeowner sees it clearly
Edge cases I designed for
  • Verification fails → guided re-entry
  • Payment returned → clear notice, easy retry
  • Drifting to delinquency → early, plain-language nudges
  • Recovery without eroding trust
